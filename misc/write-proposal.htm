The following elements are used when a proposal is reviewed by the NSF, therefore, a successful proposal must explicitly address all of them.
  1. What is the potential for the proposed activity to:
    1. Advance knowledge and understanding within its own field or across different fields (Intellectual Merit); and
    2. Benefit society or advance desired societal outcomes (Broader Impacts)?
  2. To what extent do the proposed activities suggest and explore creative, original, or potentially transformative concepts?
  3. Is the plan for carrying out the proposed activities well-reasoned, well-organized, and based on a sound rationale?
  4. Does the plan incorporate a mechanism to assess success?
  5. How well qualified is the individual, team, or organization to conduct the proposed activities?
  6. Are there adequate resources available to the PI (either at the home organization or through collaborations) to carry out the proposed activities?
Intellectual Merits
  1. Important/significance of the project
  2. Qualitification of PIs
  3. Creativity/originality/innovation/transformative potential of the methods and approaches -- e.g. move the field into a new direction (transformative), or advance the state-of-the-art in a novel and innovative way
  4. Well-conceived and organized methods and approaches
  5. Resource adaquacy
Broader Impacts
  1. Advance discovery and understanding while promoting teaching and learning
  2. Promote participation of under-represented groups
  3. Enhance infrastructure for research and education
